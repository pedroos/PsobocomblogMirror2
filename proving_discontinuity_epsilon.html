Continuity and syntactic infinitesimal

Mathematics

Pedro O S
August/2020

We'll try to explain the \(\varepsilon, \delta\)-definition of continuity for a function of one independent variable.

Let's take a look at the formal definition of continuity at a point:

A function \(f:A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) defined for the set \(A \subset \mathbb{R}\) is continuous at the point \(a \in \mathbb{R}\) when, for all \(\varepsilon \gt 0\), there is a \(\delta \gt 0\) such that \(x \in A, \vert x-a\vert \lt \delta\) implies \(\vert f(x)-f(a)\vert \lt \varepsilon\).

One way to interpret this statement informally is to say there should be no gaps in the image of the function between any two points in the domain of the function.

Another way is to say the function is discontinuous at a point, if there is a growth between it and another "close enough" point that is larger than an infinitesimally small \(\varepsilon\).

Discontinuity only occurs in functions defined over infinite sets.


Fig. 1. Discontinuous function at \(x=b\)

In the above graph, the function at \(b\) is discontinuous, and at \(a\), continuous.

Visually, we can spot the gap, but how to make it formal? In other words, how to give a precise definition, irreducible and general?

We use \(\varepsilon\) as a measure of an infinitely small quantity, by setting it to \(\gt 0\). The actual value of \(\varepsilon\) doesn't matter.

Then, start opening intervals in the function domain and checking the change in the function's image: over any but one intervals in the domain, the change in the function's image could be anything: it could be lesser or greater than a chosen \(\varepsilon\).

However, close enough to the point checked for discontinuity, the function will be expected to change by less than any number greater than zero.

"Close enough" is a terminology in Analysis for taking a point "the closest we can get" or "the closest we need to get" to another point, to observe a behavior, as opposed to "next point" or "neighbor point", applicable to a countable set.

This expectation represents the expectation that the "next" (virtual) point in the infinite string of points that compose the continuous line is there. Since this is not a countable set, the check is performed as there being any, or there existing a point, in the function's image inside an "any size greater than zero" interval. By choosing a value for \(\varepsilon\), a number larger zero, but infinitely small, is created. "Choosing a value" for \(\varepsilon\) is represented by the quantification "for all \(\varepsilon \gt 0\)".

After opening an infinitely small interval in the domain, we check that the corresponding jump is the function's image is also an infinitely small interval. If not, the jump is an actual "number", with a "length"; and represents a gap in the line and in the function.

The check for all \(\varepsilon\) to form an interval in the function image's set such that the change in the function's image is included in it is more than sufficient: any value for \(\varepsilon\) would suffice, conclusively, to determine continuity at a certain \(x\). \(\varepsilon\) is, in this sense, a variable representing an infinitely small number, which is not evident in the definition of continuity, from the need that all \(\varepsilon\) satisfy a condition: the forall quantifier is, in this case, guaranteeing that the smallest possible \(\varepsilon\) is considered, rather than guaranteening that all \(\varepsilon\)s are considered. If this would not be the case, a large value for \(\varepsilon\) where the entirety of the function's image, for example, would be included, could be chosen.

The infinitesimal, as I know it, was a quantity defined to be an infinitely small quantity. It seems to have been unfavored because of a connotation of representing a length, or not being rigorously definable. But I argue for its syntactic usefulness. Let's observe that, beyond \(\varepsilon\), \(\delta\) also represents an infinitely small quantity (\(n \gt 0 \in \mathbb{R}\)) and substitute these terms by infinitesimals in the definition of continuity.

A function \(f:A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) defined for the set \(A \subset \mathbb{R}\) is continuous at the point \(a \in \mathbb{R}\) when \(x \in A, \vert x-a\vert \lt\) infinitesimal implies \(\vert f(x)-f(a)\vert \lt\) infinitesimal.

Syntactically, this seems to let go of the existential assertion of \(\delta > 0 \in \mathbb{R}\), which seems to be an always-existant point anyway, and I'd argue makes clearer the actual work implied by the definition: that each infinitely small change in the domain should produce a corresponding infinitely small change in the image. (The quantified-expressions had me treading the wrong path: they for some reason remind me of processes rather than variables.)

The infinitesimal can't be rigorously defined:

$$\text{infinitesimal}=n>0 \in \mathbb{R} \mid n \lt m, \forall m \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}$$

As soon as an \(n\) is fixed, a smaller number is observed in the real line, so this isn't a valid expression. The infinitesimal, still, could be perhaps used syntactically to bypass writing quantified \(n \gt 0\) variables when the quantifier's used solely as a tool to define an infinitely small quantity.

Addendum (Sep. 20): Using the original definition, it is necessary to check every \(\varepsilon\) for a single \(\delta\) which features the property that for all points in the domain in between it and the point checked for continuity, the corresponding change in the image of the function is smaller than \(\varepsilon\).

Let's follow the steps.

  1. A point \(b\) is chosen.
  2. An \(\varepsilon\) is fixed.
  3. \(x\)-intervals are opened around \(b\).
  4. We want all \(x_n\)-subintervals 'contained' in our \(x\)-interval to produce changes in the function that are smaller than \(\varepsilon\).
  5. If there is one such \(x\)-interval where all \(x_n\)-subintervals have this property, then the \(\varepsilon\) 'passed' the test.
  6. If all \(\varepsilon\)s pass, the function is continuous at \(b\). If one doesn't pass, the function is discontinuous at \(b\).

This definition justifies \(\text{for all }\varepsilon \gt 0\) and \(\text{exists }\delta \gt 0\). Let's analyze it.

First, since \(\varepsilon\) is universally quantified, we can't prove continuity: we must prove discontinuity by choosing a problematic \(\varepsilon\). (We can't test every \(\varepsilon\) because there are infinitely many of them.)

Second, after \(\varepsilon\) is chosen, we want to find one \(\delta\) for which every \(x_n\)-subinterval has the property. The "every" is another universal quantification: that of every \(x_n\) in the domain. Thus, again every \(x_n\) can't be verified: there are infinitely many of them. The only hope is to find a problematic \(x_n\); in this case, one for which the jump in the image in its interval is larger than or equal to \(\varepsilon\).

Still, finding a problematic \(x_n\) would only invalidate a single candidate \(\delta\). Arbitrarily many failed \(\delta\)s could be found: fix an \(\varepsilon\) and move far away to one side with the \(\delta\) to find a point in the function where its difference to the function at \(b\) is higher than \(\varepsilon\). Generally it would be expected that by moving far away enough in a non-constant function, such difference would be found, so this is trivial. But how much far away is far away enough? The answer is that any far away could be far away enough; the function could grow fast enough around \(b\) (and still be continuous). Thus we're only interested in the close enough point to \(b\): if the function immediately grows by more then \(\varepsilon\), then that's a real discontinuity. Note this is immediately, not quickly, around \(b\). Also, we ask what \(\varepsilon\) is good enough? Similarly, the answer is any \(\varepsilon\) could not be enough as a ruler. By increasing \(\varepsilon\), we allow greater variation in the function to pass as continuous. The point here is to examine the smallest possible \(\varepsilon\): if the change is larger than that, then that's a real discontinuity. The question may be asked, "why not larger than zero then?" The answer is "because the expected change is larger than zero: it is a minimum change". If all expected change was zero, only constant functions would pass as continuous, obviously. The only way to express an infinitely small value, larger than zero, without defining infinitesimal, is using the expression all quantities greater than zero, which includes the infinitely small quantity in it. This is the purpose of these quantified variables.

In other words,

  • If a big enough \(\varepsilon\) is chosen, any change in the function could fit in it. So, obviously, we're not interested in arbitrary \(\varepsilon\)s. They are meaningless. Neither are we interested in all \(\varepsilon\)s: these contain all arbitrary \(\varepsilon\)s. What remains is the infinitely small \(\varepsilon\).
  • If a big enough \(\delta\) is chosen, any change in the function could be found for some \(x\)-subinterval in it. So, similarly, we would not interested in arbitrary \(\delta\), but unlike \(\varepsilon\), \(\delta\) is existentially quantified. This means any particular one of them is not interesting, as long as one is considered. Their value is meaningless. The only relevant property that remains, then, is that of being \(\gt 0\), meaning they refer to the infinitely small quantity.

Addendum (Oct/20): It is possible "go in reverse" in a sense and begin by observing that the resulting distance in the image must be smaller than \(\varepsilon\) for all \(\varepsilon \in \mathbb{R}\). This is itself informs we're looking for the smallest Real larger than zero (and nothing else).

From that, we consider which change in the domain we're examining for such infinitesimally small change in the image. It could be three things, in terms of distance:

  1. The zero change;
  2. The infinitesimally small change;
  3. Any other change, meaning an arbitrary non-zero and non-infinitesimally small Real.

Let's examine each possibility.

a) is trivially non-interesting.

c) is non-interesting because an arbitrary change in the image of the function at an arbitrarily distance in the domain could just be from the function's normal course; the function could still be continuous between the point and it.

b) is what remains.

This is enough to conclude the definition is only about looking at the change in the image from the infinitesimally small change in the domain, and checking it's also infinitesimally small.

This characterizes this form of definition as analogous to definining:

1. "The terrain around where someone's at is flat in a direction (not considering the Earth's curvature) if, for all numbers of steps taken in that direction, there is one number of steps that is smaller than them all and taking this number of steps implies that, for all possible changes in the terrain, there is a change in terrain that is smaller than them all and this is the change observed."

Where this definition could be instead phrased:

2. "The terrain around where somebody's at is flat in a direction (not considering the Earth's curvature if) if the smallest possible step is taken in that direction, and the change in the terrain is the smallest possible."

The definition 1) is employed because "smallest" is not logically definable in the theory in question (Analysis): there is only formally the concept of "smaller than". So, to be rigorous, 1) has to be employed.

Correction: smallest is definable for closed sets; but not for an open set like the set of numbers greater than zero — the set in question — for example.

However, if we define "smallest" non-logically, that is, as a "figure of language" of sorts, than we can phrase theorems in Analysis in the form 2) instead of 1).

This seems, to me, first, more a problem of examining why there doesn't exist, or can't exist, a "smallest"; than of using an elaborated construction to work around it, even in a formal setting.

Addendum (Nov. 20): After writing, I had an initial look at non-standard analysis, which seems to be a branch of Mathematics proposing to formalize the Infinitesimal.

I came to it (re-visited it, actually) with the hope that it would try to solve primarily from a pedagogical perspective, but it seems not to be the case. To be rigorous, it extends the number system, which I'm already wary of 😀.

By the way: finally understading Emojis as fully formal characters and not some additional layer, I shall feel free to use them :).

Here is an excerpt from [1], p. 17:

An infinitesimal is a 'number' that is smaller than each positive real number and larger than each negative real number, so that in the real number system there is just one infinitesimal, i.e. zero. But most of the time only nonzero infinitesimals are of interest. This is related to the fact that when in the usual limit definition \(x\) is tending to \(c\), most of the time only the values of \(x\) that are different from \(c\) are of interest. Hence the real number system has to be extended in some way or other in order to include all infinitesimals.

My informal, didactic idea adopted instead a single, constant infinitesimal larger than zero and smaller than any other real number.

From [2]:

These new numbers will have the property that although different from \(0\), each is smaller than every positive real number and larger than every negative real number. (...) These new infinitesimals, once suitably defined, will enable us to solve general problems of slopes of tangents and areas of regions with extraordinary ease.

At a point, it becomes necessary to look at the "why" of defining number systems; there seems to be a point where it becomes more about the behaviors and operations than about quantities. Which I'd argue is happening as early as the real numbers, maybe. This will be touched upon in a future article.

Addendum (Dec. 20): In the very informative work [3], the author gives historical context and an introduction to smooth infinitesimal analysis (SIA). (SIA is a sistematization of infinitesimals into a number system.) We'll comment on some passages of note.

Cauchy mentions a variable quantity with numerical values (...) in a 'dynamic' way, unlike the 'static' type of Leibniz and Euler, where \(\mathop{dx}\) is a fixed, if indeterminate, variable.

And the continuation:

Cauchy uses small numbers \(\delta\) and \(\varepsilon\). The extensive use of the 'delta-epsilon' form of analysis originates with Weierstra√ü (1815-1897), who introduced most of the rigorous foundations of analysis still taught today.

About the first quote, type seems an appropriate word to characterize the variety of infinitesimally small quantities in consideration. Now taken in a slightly more formal 'programming language' context, it helps me to say the \(\delta, \varepsilon\) statement form seems obviously "less typed" than a symbol form such as \(\mathop{dx}\). In a programming language, symbols are used to represent concepts to obviate the need to repeat statements. In an object-oriented language, if a symbol is not only a quantity, but an object, then it has a class which describes behavior around the object (the object is said to have a "type"). This would be more-or-less what a \(\mathop{dx}\) symbol would achieve in mathematical notation, as opposed to a "verbatim" \(\delta, \varepsilon\) statement. Roughly, this corresponds to the distinction between procedural and object-oriented programming, where the latter is given as a solution to the former; a form to structure behavior around concepts that provide important semantic advantages.

The Weierstra√ü formalization is identified with a context and period of formalization of, respectively, Functions by Cauchy, and Sets by Cantor. It is notable that \(\delta, \varepsilon\) allows limit definition by the order relation \(\lt\), where with the Set definition of number systems it is now possible to use the same order relation \(\lt\) for integer, fractional or irrational numbers; that is, real numbers under the same classification. Defining functions as relations between Sets now brings the order relation \(\lt\) within the realm of functions, whereas with a static (that is, not as a number) definition of infinitesimally small quantity, such as \(\mathop{dx}\), such quantity is not directly comparable with numbers by the order relation \(\lt\), which seems to be the "problem of formality" with it, in fact, already in Leibniz:

Leibniz was aware of the criticism he would get for using inÔ¨Ånitesimals, so in his papers, he hid their use and introduced \(\mathop{dx}\) as a Ô¨Ånite quantity, arbitrarily Ô¨Åxed a priori.

This \(\lt\) analysis is meant to say \(\mathop{dx}\) is not formal, inasmuch as the Set definition of numbers as related by the order relation \(\lt\) is formal. This is not to say this number system definition is not formal; but that in a hypothetic system where it is not used and numbers are not defined in this way (or not defined at all!), then this "problem" with infinitesimals is lifted; that is, the \(\lt\) comparison seems to be the only dependence of formality of such construction in relation to this number system.

At the time, the concept of functions was not yet conceived. Leibniz considered a curve to be a polygon with an inÔ¨Ånite number of inÔ¨Ånitesimal sides, an infinitangular polygon.

I'm now more suspect of mentions of formalization, because I now read "formalization" as "formalization into the Set form of the real number system", where before I read it as inclusion into some kind of truth. Turns out, the real number system has a history of its own, and the geometric perspective in play into Leibniz's theories bears investigation because of classical geometry being an example of a pre-Set form of real number system which appears to already contain a lot of the problematique of the post-Set form of real number system.

For example, the above cited characterization of a curve, which I fully appreciate, is analogous to the circle as polygon — an infinite-sided polygon which, nevertheless by having a larger or smaller quantity of infinite sides by virtue of being a larger or smaller diameter circle, contains the "passage into the uncountable"; that is, the possibility of many-sized infinities.

In which way geometry is distinct from the real number system? To make it clear, since it has no concept of sets or functions, it "barely" can be said to contain a concept of "number" as well; as we all know, "numbers" in geometry are taken to be measurements, and irrationals are, by not being measurable, considered distinct from numbers as measurements. It could well be taken that "numbers" in this system could be conceived as referring to the measurable quantities only, and the immeasurable quantities considered other kinds of entities entirely. This'd free the system from the need to unify the entities as "numbers" (maybe even save the chap who discovered irrationals in Greeceüò≠). Nevertheless, geometry can, and is, still used to solve problems — even continuous ones — the fact that Leibniz's Calculus as being of geometrical nature furnishing evidence of so.

Such system could maybe be shown to be more amenable to definition in an Object Oriented way (as much as a science of Object Orientation exists); thus offering the possibility of freedom of organization into its own structure. The question poses itself how much could such a system be "stretched" in its sophistication, so as to give solutions to ever more complicated problems?
